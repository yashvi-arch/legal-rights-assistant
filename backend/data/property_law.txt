PROPERTY & HOUSING LAW (India)

1. TRANSFER OF PROPERTY ACT, 1882
Governs how property (land, buildings) can be legally transferred through sale, mortgage, lease, gift, or exchange. Any transfer must be through a registered, properly executed document to be legally valid.

2. REGISTRATION ACT, 1908
Documents related to property transactions worth more than Rs. 100 (in practice, virtually all property sales) must be registered with the local Sub-Registrar's office to be legally enforceable. An unregistered sale deed generally cannot be used to claim ownership in court.

3. REAL ESTATE (REGULATION AND DEVELOPMENT) ACT, 2016 (RERA)
Protects homebuyers from builder delays, fraud, and poor construction quality. Builders must register projects with the state RERA authority, disclose project details, and deposit 70% of buyer payments in an escrow account for construction costs. Buyers can file complaints with the state RERA for delays or defects.

4. RENT CONTROL ACTS (State-specific)
Most states have their own Rent Control Act regulating rent increases, eviction grounds, and security deposits. Landlords generally cannot evict tenants without valid legal grounds (like non-payment of rent or personal need) and must follow due process through a Rent Controller or civil court.

5. MODEL TENANCY ACT, 2021 (template for states to adopt)
Proposes capping security deposits at 2 months' rent for residential and 6 months for commercial properties, mandatory written rental agreements, and timely return of deposits. Not all states have adopted this yet — actual rules depend on your state's specific Rent Control Act.

6. RIGHT TO FAIR COMPENSATION AND TRANSPARENCY IN LAND ACQUISITION ACT, 2013
When the government acquires private land for public projects, it must pay fair compensation (based on market value) and conduct a Social Impact Assessment. Affected families are also entitled to rehabilitation and resettlement benefits.

7. INDIAN EASEMENTS ACT, 1882
Governs rights like right of way, right to light, and right to water that one property owner may have over a neighboring property.

8. PARTITION OF PROPERTY
Co-owners of inherited property (like siblings) have the right to seek partition (division) of jointly-owned property either through mutual agreement or a partition suit in civil court if they cannot agree.

9. ADVERSE POSSESSION
Under Indian law, if someone occupies land continuously, openly, and without the owner's permission for 12 years (government land: 30 years), they may be able to claim legal ownership — though this is increasingly difficult to establish and courts apply strict scrutiny.

10. HOMEBUYER GRIEVANCE REDRESSAL
Apart from RERA, homebuyers can also approach Consumer Courts under the Consumer Protection Act for builder deficiencies, or civil courts for breach of contract.

How to seek help: File complaints with your state's RERA authority (search "[your state] RERA") for builder issues, or approach the Rent Controller's office or civil court for landlord-tenant disputes. For property registration queries, visit your local Sub-Registrar office.
