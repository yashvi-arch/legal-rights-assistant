FUNDAMENTAL RIGHTS & CONSTITUTIONAL LAW (India)

1. ARTICLE 14 — RIGHT TO EQUALITY
The State cannot deny any person equality before the law or equal protection of the laws. This means similar treatment for people in similar circumstances, though reasonable classification (like reservations) is allowed.

2. ARTICLE 15 — PROHIBITION OF DISCRIMINATION
The State cannot discriminate against any citizen on grounds of religion, race, caste, sex, or place of birth. Special provisions for women, children, and socially/educationally backward classes are allowed (like reservations).

3. ARTICLE 19 — RIGHT TO FREEDOM
Guarantees freedom of speech and expression, peaceful assembly, forming associations, movement throughout India, residing anywhere in India, and practicing any profession or trade — subject to reasonable restrictions (like public order, security, decency).

4. ARTICLE 21 — RIGHT TO LIFE AND PERSONAL LIBERTY
No person can be deprived of life or personal liberty except through a fair legal procedure. Courts have expanded this to include the right to privacy, right to a clean environment, right to livelihood, and right to a speedy trial.

5. ARTICLE 21A — RIGHT TO EDUCATION
Free and compulsory education is a fundamental right for all children aged 6-14, implemented through the Right of Children to Free and Compulsory Education Act, 2009 (RTE Act).

6. ARTICLE 22 — PROTECTION AGAINST ARBITRARY ARREST
A person arrested must be informed of the grounds of arrest, has the right to consult a lawyer, and must be produced before a magistrate within 24 hours (excluding travel time).

7. ARTICLE 25-28 — FREEDOM OF RELIGION
Citizens have the right to freely profess, practice, and propagate any religion, subject to public order, morality, and health.

8. ARTICLE 32 — RIGHT TO CONSTITUTIONAL REMEDIES
Any citizen whose fundamental rights are violated can directly approach the Supreme Court for enforcement of those rights — this is sometimes called the "heart and soul" of the Constitution. Similarly, Article 226 allows approaching High Courts.

9. PUBLIC INTEREST LITIGATION (PIL)
Any citizen or organization can file a case in the public interest (not just for personal grievance) before the High Court or Supreme Court on matters affecting the public — like pollution, corruption, or human rights violations.

10. RIGHT TO PRIVACY (Justice K.S. Puttaswamy vs Union of India, 2017)
The Supreme Court declared the right to privacy a fundamental right under Article 21, impacting laws related to data protection, surveillance, and personal autonomy (including matters like LGBTQ+ rights and personal choices).

11. DIRECTIVE PRINCIPLES OF STATE POLICY (Part IV)
Not directly enforceable in court, but guide the government in policy-making — covering areas like equal pay, free legal aid, environmental protection, and uniform civil code.

How to seek help: For violation of fundamental rights, you can file a Writ Petition directly in the High Court (Article 226) or Supreme Court (Article 32). Legal aid is available through District Legal Services Authorities for those who cannot afford a lawyer.
