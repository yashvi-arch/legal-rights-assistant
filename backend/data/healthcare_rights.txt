HEALTHCARE RIGHTS (India)

1. RIGHT TO EMERGENCY MEDICAL TREATMENT
The Supreme Court (Paschim Banga Khet Mazdoor Samity vs State of West Bengal, 1996) ruled that hospitals — both government and private — cannot refuse emergency treatment to any patient, regardless of their ability to pay. Refusal can be challenged as a violation of Article 21 (Right to Life).

2. CLINICAL ESTABLISHMENTS (REGISTRATION AND REGULATION) ACT, 2010
Requires hospitals and clinics to display their rates for services, maintain standard treatment protocols, and register with local authorities. Many states have adopted this Act or have similar state-level regulations.

3. CONSUMER PROTECTION FOR MEDICAL NEGLIGENCE
Patients can file a complaint against doctors/hospitals for medical negligence under the Consumer Protection Act, 2019, in Consumer Courts, or pursue a criminal complaint for gross negligence under the BNS (previously IPC Section 304A).

4. RIGHT TO INFORMED CONSENT
Doctors must explain the nature of treatment, risks, alternatives, and costs before a patient consents to any procedure. Treatment without informed consent (except emergencies) can be challenged legally.

5. MENTAL HEALTHCARE ACT, 2017
Recognizes the right to access mental healthcare and treatment from government-run or government-funded health services, decriminalizes attempted suicide (treats it as a mental health crisis needing care rather than punishment), and protects the rights of persons with mental illness, including the right to make decisions about their own treatment (advance directives).

6. AYUSHMAN BHARAT / PM-JAY
A government health insurance scheme providing coverage up to Rs. 5 lakh per family per year for secondary and tertiary hospitalization, targeted at economically vulnerable families. Eligibility can be checked at pmjay.gov.in.

7. RIGHT TO MEDICAL RECORDS
Patients have a right to access their own medical records, test reports, and discharge summaries from hospitals. Hospitals are generally required to provide these within a reasonable time upon request.

8. TRANSPLANTATION OF HUMAN ORGANS AND TISSUES ACT, 1994
Regulates organ donation and transplantation, prohibiting commercial dealing in organs. Living donors can only donate to close relatives (with some exceptions requiring authorization committee approval) to prevent organ trafficking.

9. PRE-CONCEPTION AND PRE-NATAL DIAGNOSTIC TECHNIQUES (PCPNDT) ACT, 1994
Prohibits sex determination of a fetus and sex-selective abortion. Violators (including doctors and pregnant women's families in some cases) face imprisonment and fines.

10. MEDICAL TERMINATION OF PREGNANCY (MTP) ACT, 1971 (Amended 2021)
Allows abortion up to 20 weeks with the opinion of one doctor (up to 24 weeks for special categories of women, with two doctors' approval). Beyond 24 weeks, a Medical Board's approval is needed in case of substantial fetal abnormalities.

How to seek help: For hospital negligence or billing disputes, approach the District/State Consumer Disputes Redressal Commission. For mental health crisis support, the national helpline KIRAN is 1800-599-0019. For Ayushman Bharat queries, call 14555.
